Encryption Public Keys

All data can be kept private and secure using encryption. Encryption is scrambling or changing a message to hide its original text. (Decryption is the unscrambling of the message to make it readable).

Caesar’s Cipher, named after Julius Caesar, was one of the first methods of encryption. This method substitutes letters in a message with another letter a certain number of characters further down in the alphabet. The numbers of letters difference, or the key, is something only the sender and receiver know. The issue with this is, someone could easily decrypt the message by trying every possible key.

Today's communication is encrypted using 256 bit keys. Even with “super computers” that could work non stop, around the clock, it would take trillions and trillions of years to crack a single message.

When a sender and receiver use the same key to encrypt and decrypt a message, it is called symmetric encryption. The secret key is something that is agreed on ahead of time, in private. Computers use what is called asymmetric encryption, different keys for encrypting and decrypting.
